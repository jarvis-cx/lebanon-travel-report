# Lebanon Travel Safety Report — August 2026
## Northern Christian Regions: Jounieh, Byblos, Bcharre, Kesrouan/Metn

**Party:** 2 Australian adults + 2 children (ages 7 & 9) | **Duration:** 2 weeks | **Research date:** 10 March 2026

---

## ⚠️ CRITICAL UPFRONT ASSESSMENT

**As of March 10, 2026, Lebanon is in an active armed conflict. Travel is not recommended under any circumstances at this time.**

On 2 March 2026, following the US-Israel joint strike campaign against Iran (which began 28 February 2026) and the assassination of Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, Hezbollah launched strikes on Israel. Israel responded with airstrikes across Lebanon, including Beirut. A ground invasion was authorized on 3 March. As of writing, 394 people have been killed and 1,300 injured in Lebanon, with 500,000 displaced. This conflict is **ongoing and escalating**.

All three major Western governments have issued their highest or near-highest travel warnings. **This report is written to inform a decision about travel 5 months from now (August 2026). The situation may improve dramatically, or worsen further. The report covers both the current crisis and the baseline conditions that would apply if/when the conflict resolves.**

## 1. Current Security Situation

### The March 2026 Escalation
- **28 Feb 2026:** US and Israel launched joint strikes against Iran ("2026 Iran war")
- **2 Mar 2026:** Hezbollah launched retaliatory strikes on northern Israel following assassination of Ali Khamenei
- **2-3 Mar 2026:** Israel responded with airstrikes on Beirut, southern Lebanon, and Baalbek-Hermel
- **3 Mar 2026:** Israel authorized ground invasion of Lebanon
- **4 Mar 2026:** IDF issued evacuation warnings for 13 villages; ordered everyone south of Litani River to leave; issued warnings for Beirut's southern suburbs
- **As of 10 Mar 2026:** Conflict ongoing. 394 killed, 1,300 injured, 500,000 displaced in Lebanon
- **Source:** Wikipedia "2026 Hezbollah–Israel war"; ACLED March 2026 special issue; UK House of Commons Library briefing (9 Mar 2026)

### Background (Pre-March 2026)
- Nov 2024: US-brokered ceasefire between Israel and Hezbollah after Israel invaded southern Lebanon and killed Hezbollah leader Hassan Nasrallah
- Feb 2025: IDF withdrew from most of southern Lebanon, retaining 5 strategic border posts
- Throughout 2025: Near-daily Israeli ceasefire violations (500+ airstrikes per OHCHR); LAF made progress disarming Hezbollah south of Litani
- End 2025: Deadline for Hezbollah disarmament south of Litani. Lebanese government said "nearly done" but Israel said "insufficient"
- Jan 2026: Fragile situation — LAF unable to operate near Israeli-occupied points
- Sep 2025: Australia had *downgraded* advisory from "Do Not Travel" to "Reconsider" — brief window of optimism

### Northern Christian Regions vs South
- **Jounieh, Byblos (Jbeil), Bcharre, Kesrouan, Metn** are in Mount Lebanon Governorate and North Governorate — historically the safest areas of Lebanon
- These are predominantly Christian areas, with no significant Hezbollah military presence
- During the 2024 conflict, these areas were largely untouched by airstrikes
- **However:** In the current March 2026 escalation, airstrikes have hit Beirut (the gateway airport is there), and mass displacement means these areas are absorbing refugees
- Bcharre and the Qadisha Valley are remote mountain towns — very far from any conflict zone
- **Assessment:** Even in the worst periods, northern Christian Lebanon has been physically safe. The risk is proximity to infrastructure targets, airport closures, and being unable to evacuate

### August 2026 Outlook
- **Impossible to predict.** The 2024 conflict led to a ceasefire within ~2 months. If this follows a similar pattern, a ceasefire could be in place by May-June 2026
- Key factors: Whether US-Iran war de-escalates; whether Hezbollah's capacity is further degraded; whether a new ceasefire is brokered
- **Recommendation:** Do not book until at least May 2026. Monitor weekly. The situation in northern Lebanon specifically could be safe long before southern Lebanon

### Analytical Assessment
All three governments are at maximum or near-maximum alert. The timing matters: advisories were already elevated *before* the March 2 escalation. They will almost certainly remain elevated through at least Q2 2026. For August travel, you'd need to see:

## 3. Entry Requirements

### Visa for Australian Passport Holders
- **Visa on arrival** available for Australian citizens at Beirut-Rafic Hariri International Airport
- Free 1-month visa stamped on arrival (extendable to 3 months at General Security offices)
- Passport must be valid for at least 6 months beyond date of entry
- No eVisa system required — it's simply a stamp at immigration
- **Children:** Same requirements; each child needs their own passport

### Israel Stamp Policy
- **Critical:** Lebanon refuses entry to anyone with Israeli stamps, visas, or evidence of travel to Israel in their passport
- Israel no longer stamps passports (since 2013, they use a paper slip), but any Israeli visa sticker, or entry/exit stamps from land borders with Jordan/Egypt near Israel, could cause issues
- **For Australians:** If you've visited Israel, ensure your passport has no evidence. If in doubt, renew your passport before travel

### Beirut Airport Experience (Pre-March 2026)
- Beirut-Rafic Hariri International Airport is the only commercial airport
- Immigration can be slow (30-60 min queues) but straightforward for Australians
- Airport is in southern Beirut suburbs — this area has been subject to airstrikes and evacuation orders in March 2026
- **Current status (March 2026):** Airport is "open and operational" per FCDO, but security situation is extremely fluid. Airlines may cancel flights at short notice.

## 4. Health & Medical

### Vaccinations
- **Required:** None for entry from Australia
- **Recommended (CDC/WHO):** Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B, Typhoid, routine vaccinations (MMR, DTPa, Polio) should be up to date
- Consider Rabies if children will be around stray animals (common in rural Lebanon)
- COVID-19: No specific requirements as of 2025/26

### Water & Food Safety
- **Tap water is NOT safe to drink** anywhere in Lebanon. Use bottled water exclusively, including for brushing teeth
- Street food in Jounieh/Byblos is generally safe if cooked fresh and from busy vendors
- Restaurants in Christian areas are generally hygienic — standard Mediterranean/Middle Eastern food safety applies
- Ice in restaurants: variable quality — stick to known establishments

### Hospitals in Northern Lebanon
- **Notre Dame Hospital, Jounieh** — Good general hospital in the Kesrouan area
- **Notre Dame Maritime Hospital, Byblos (Jbeil)** — Near the coast
- **Saint Georges Hospital, Ajaltoun** — Mount Lebanon area
- **Bcharre:** Very limited medical facilities; nearest significant hospital would be in Tripoli or back toward Jounieh (2+ hours drive)
- Lebanon historically had excellent medical care (medical tourism destination), but the economic crisis since 2019 has caused an exodus of medical professionals, drug shortages, and reduced hospital capacity (per UNICEF)
- **Private hospitals** still function well for those who can pay in USD

### Travel Insurance
- **Essential.** Must cover medical evacuation (medevac to Cyprus or Turkey — nearest advanced care)
- **Critical issue:** Most travel insurance policies will NOT cover you if you travel against government advice (currently "Do Not Travel"). This is a dealbreaker until advisories change
- Budget for comprehensive family policy with medevac: ~AUD $500-800 for 2 weeks, family of 4

### Children in August Heat
- August is the hottest month: 30-32°C on the coast (Jounieh/Byblos), high humidity (70-80%)
- Bcharre in the mountains: significantly cooler, 25-28°C
- **For ages 7 and 9:** Ensure constant hydration, sun protection (SPF 50+), avoid midday sun (12-3pm). The heat is serious but manageable with planning
- Gastro risks are higher for children — stick to bottled water, cooked food, avoid salads washed in tap water

## 5. Family Travel

### Child-Friendly Attractions in Northern Lebanon

**Jounieh/Kesrouan:**
- **Jeita Grotto** — Spectacular limestone caves with stalactites/stalagmites. Upper grotto on foot, lower grotto by boat. Kids love it. One of Lebanon's top attractions. ~20 min from Jounieh
- **Téléférique (Cable Car) to Harissa** — Cable car ride from Jounieh bay up to the Our Lady of Lebanon statue. Stunning views. Kids enjoy the ride. ~10 min each way
- **Jounieh Bay beaches** — Sandy beaches, warm water in August. Beach clubs with facilities

**Byblos (Jbeil):**
- **Byblos Crusader Castle & Archaeological Site** — One of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world (~7,000 years). Kids can explore ruins, castle walls. UNESCO World Heritage Site
- **Byblos Old Souk** — Charming market streets, ice cream, crafts
- **Byblos Fishing Harbour** — Picturesque, good for walking and photos
- **Bahsa Beach and nearby beach clubs**

**Bcharre/Qadisha:**
- **Cedars of God (Arz el-Rab)** — Ancient cedar forest, UNESCO World Heritage. Cooler mountain air. Easy walking trails suitable for children
- **Qadisha Valley** — Dramatic gorge with monasteries. Hiking trails (some challenging — choose carefully with young kids)
- **Gibran Museum** — Dedicated to Khalil Gibran, housed in a former monastery. Educational for older children
- **Baatara Gorge Waterfall** — Spectacular 3-tier waterfall, ~1 hour from Bcharre. Viewing only (steep terrain)

### Cultural Norms Around Children
- Lebanese culture is extremely child-friendly. Children are welcomed everywhere, including restaurants late at night
- Strangers may interact warmly with your children — touching their hair, giving them sweets. This is cultural affection, not a threat
- Christian areas are relaxed about dress codes; modest clothing appreciated but not required
- No specific safety concerns for Western children

## 6. Getting Around

### Distances & Drive Times (Northern Route)
| Route | Distance | Drive Time |
|-------|----------|------------|
| Beirut Airport → Jounieh | ~20 km | 30-60 min (traffic dependent) |
| Jounieh → Byblos | ~18 km | 20-30 min |
| Byblos → Bcharre | ~75 km | 1.5-2.5 hours (mountain roads) |
| Jounieh → Bcharre | ~90 km | 2-3 hours |
| Byblos → Batroun | ~15 km | 15-20 min |

### Car Hire
- Available from Beirut airport and Jounieh: Sixt, Europcar, local companies (carsrentallebanon.com etc.)
- Prices: ~$30-60/day for a mid-size SUV (recommended for mountain roads)
- **International driving permit** required alongside Australian licence
- Lebanese roads along the coast (Route 51M, the main highway) are good quality
- Mountain roads to Bcharre: winding, narrow in parts, but paved. Scenic but demanding driving
- **Driving culture:** Aggressive. Lane markings are suggestions. Horn use is constant. Overtaking on blind corners happens. Night driving on mountain roads is not recommended
- Child car seats: Bring your own or confirm with rental company — availability is inconsistent

### Taxis & Ride-Hailing
- **Bolt** is the main ride-hailing app (Uber was available but inconsistent)
- Traditional taxis available; negotiate price before departure
- Shared "service" taxis run common routes cheaply
- For airport transfer: pre-book through hotel — safer and easier with kids and luggage

### Road Safety Assessment
- Lebanon has one of the highest road fatality rates in the region
- Police presence is minimal; traffic laws are loosely enforced
- **With children, a private driver/car with driver is worth considering** — many hotels can arrange this. Cost: ~$80-150/day for a driver with car

## 7. Where to Stay

### Recommended Areas

**Jounieh (Best base for families)**
- Coastal town 20km north of Beirut. Christian area, lively, good infrastructure
- Close to Jeita Grotto, Harissa, easy access to Byblos
- Hotel range: Budget $20-50/night, Mid-range $70-120/night, Upscale $150-250/night
- Family-friendly options with pools are available

**Byblos (Jbeil) (Best for charm)**
- Historic port town with UNESCO site. Quieter than Jounieh
- Hotels from $67/night (budget) to $200+/night (boutique)
- Fewer family-specific amenities but beautiful setting
- Walking distance to archaeological sites, harbour, restaurants

**Bcharre (Mountain escape)**
- Mountain town at 1,400m elevation. Cool in August. Gateway to Cedars and Qadisha Valley
- Limited accommodation — small hotels and guesthouses, $40-100/night
- Much quieter, less touristy. Good for 3-4 nights within a 2-week trip

**Batroun (Honorable mention)**
- Coastal town between Byblos and Tripoli. Beach clubs, old churches, craft beer scene
- Emerging as a family destination. Worth a day trip or overnight

### Areas to Avoid
- **Southern Beirut suburbs** (Dahieh) — Hezbollah stronghold, active conflict zone
- **South of Saida** — Active military zone
- **Beqaa Valley / Baalbek** — Despite spectacular ruins, FCDO advises against all travel
- **Tripoli** — FCDO advises against all travel; historically unstable
- **Any Palestinian refugee camps** — All advisories say avoid
- **Akkar** — Northern border region, unstable

### Suggested Itinerary Split (2 Weeks)
- Jounieh base: 7 nights (day trips to Jeita, Harissa, Beirut if safe)
- Byblos: 3-4 nights
- Bcharre/Cedars: 3-4 nights

## 8. Money & Economy

### Currency Situation (As of March 2026)
- **Official exchange rate:** ~89,500 LBP per 1 USD (fixed by Banque du Liban since ~2023)
- Market rate: approximately 89,000-90,000 LBP per USD — now largely aligned with official rate after years of crisis
- The Lebanese Pound has been effectively dollarized — **USD is the de facto currency**
- Most businesses price in USD and prefer USD cash
- **Pre-crisis rate was 1,500 LBP per USD** — the currency lost ~98% of its value

### Practical Money Advice
- **Bring USD cash.** This is the most important financial advice. Crisp, new bills preferred ($50 and $100 denominations)
- ATMs: Unreliable for foreign cards. Some may dispense USD but at poor rates. Do not rely on ATMs
- Credit cards: Accepted at major hotels and upscale restaurants, but many businesses are cash-only
- Money changers: Available everywhere; rates are standardized now
- **Do not bring Lebanese pounds from abroad** — they're essentially worthless relative to USD

### Daily Budget (Family of 4, USD)

## 10. August-Specific Factors

### Weather
- **Coast (Jounieh, Byblos):** 27-32°C, humid (70-80%), virtually no rain. Hot but tempered by sea breeze
- **Mountains (Bcharre, 1,400m):** 22-28°C, much drier, pleasant. Can be cool in evenings
- **UV Index:** Very high (8-10). Sunburn risk is serious
- August is statistically the hottest month but also essentially rain-free

### Festivals & Events
- **Byblos International Festival:** Major music festival held annually in July-August in the Crusader castle ruins. Returned in 2023 after a 3-year hiatus; 2025 edition was held. International and Lebanese artists. Amazing setting, family-friendly earlier shows. Check byblosfestival.com for 2026 lineup/dates
- **Jounieh International Festival:** Usually July. Fireworks, concerts on the bay
- **Assumption of Mary (15 August):** Major holiday in Christian Lebanon. Celebrations, processions, church services throughout Jounieh, Byblos, Bcharre. Festive atmosphere

### Tourist Season
- August is peak Lebanese tourist season — many diaspora Lebanese return for summer
- Hotels in Byblos/Jounieh will be at premium pricing and higher occupancy
- Book accommodation early if proceeding
- Beach clubs will be crowded on weekends
- Mountain areas (Bcharre/Cedars) are a popular escape from coastal heat — expect visitors

### Security Patterns in August
- Historically, August has not been a period of specific security risk in northern Lebanon
- The 2006 Israel-Hezbollah war ran July-August — but that was an anomaly and directly targeted southern Lebanon
- The current March 2026 escalation is the most significant security concern
- **If a ceasefire is achieved by May-June 2026, August travel to northern Christian areas could be viable** — this was the pattern after the Nov 2024 ceasefire

## Overall Assessment & Recommendation

### The Honest Take

**Right now (March 2026): Absolutely do not go.** Active war, all governments at "Do Not Travel," airport access uncertain.

**For August 2026: It depends entirely on how the next 3-4 months unfold.** Here's the decision framework:

#### ✅ GO Signals (all must be true):
1. A ceasefire is in place and holding for 2+ months
2. Australian advisory downgraded to at least "Reconsider" (orange)
3. Beirut airport operating normally with multiple airlines
4. You can obtain comprehensive travel insurance (impossible under "Do Not Travel")
5. Northern Lebanon specifically is not subject to any military activity

#### ❌ NO-GO Signals (any one = cancel):
1. Advisory remains "Do Not Travel" in July 2026
2. Active airstrikes anywhere in Lebanon
3. Beirut airport operations disrupted
4. Unable to get travel insurance
5. Australian Embassy Beirut is closed or evacuated

#### The Northern Lebanon Advantage
If conditions stabilize, the northern Christian areas (Jounieh, Byblos, Bcharre) are genuinely among the safest places in the Levant. They were safe through the 2006 war, through the 2019-2023 economic collapse, and through the 2024 conflict. The risk is never *being in* northern Lebanon — it's getting in and out through Beirut, and being trapped if things escalate suddenly.

#### With Children
Taking children aged 7 and 9 raises the stakes. The trip itself (Jeita Grotto, Cedars, Byblos ruins, cable car, beaches) would be absolutely magical for kids. But the evacuation risk is real. Commercial flights are the only way in/out, and they can be cancelled at hours' notice.

#### Practical Next Steps
1. **Wait until May 2026** before making any booking decisions
2. **Book refundable flights and accommodation only**
3. **Register with Smartraveller** before departure
4. **Have an evacuation plan** — know ferry routes to Cyprus, overland to Syria/Turkey (if borders open)
5. **Bring sufficient USD cash** ($3,000-5,000 for 2 weeks, family of 4)
6. **Consider trip duration** — 1 week instead of 2 reduces risk exposure
